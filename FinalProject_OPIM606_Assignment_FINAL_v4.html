Overview:

The year 2020 marked a momentous year for this nations history. Not only was there a - highly polarizing - presidential election, but multiple human-rights movements domestically/internationally, and more importantly, an international pandemic took place. Historically, presidential elections are marked as major catalysts for market movement. As each candidate carries unique ideologies and methodologies, they can have astronomical influence on the individual values of a stock. For instance, when the presidency transitioned from President George W. Bush to Barack Obama, the value of solar/EV (i.e., Tesla) related stocks skyrocketed in valuation. This is simply due to President Barack Obamas heavy emphasis in reducing greenhouse emissions and becoming a global leader in solar energy. In addition to the political factors, a social revolution took place in 2020 that affected the capital markets. This includes civil unrests and protests across the globe that affected market certainty. Lastly, the year 2020 endured a pandemic that caused the entire planet to cease. Consequently, the world suffered supply and demand-related issues that affected the near-future outlook of the global economy.

In this study, the Georgetown team analyzed the effects of politics / political event(s) on the US stock market. The team is specifically looking at Vice President Joe Biden and President Donald Trumps election probability from 2020. The team hypothesized that most companies (as they are incorporated in the DJIA) will have a positive relationship with a candidates election/re-election probability. The team also hypothesized that the stock market will benefit when the winner of general election becomes evident.

After accounting for the first order autocorrelations using Cochrane-Orcutt estimation, we can see that the time component of the stocks is an important variable to consider when running linear models to examine the associations between closing price of the presidents and the closing price of a stock. After using Cochrane-Orcutt estimation, many of the associations that were previously significant are no longer significant.

One limitation of this study was not being able to truly use time-series analysis to examine the associations between a presidents closing time and the closing price of a stock while taking into account the time variable (date).

Significant Stocks vs Presidents and Major Events

The final step the team took was to analyze the eight stocks discovered from the previous step. The goal of this was to see the relationship between individual securities against factors related to both Donald Trump and Joe Biden winning the election including: 1. Difference in election probability per day (Biden % - Trump %) described as Winning % Difference and 2. Trade-Volume Difference described as Trade Volume Difference. As shown in the graph, many of the spikes of 2020 are aligned with some of the major events that occurred, including the Presidential Impeachment, BLM movement, and COVID Vaccine Roll-out to name a few. In addition, the graph suggests that the market did not initially take comfort in Joe Biden being the likely candidate (shown by the 3rd and 4th vertical line) but as the year went on, the market displayed positive correlation with the likelihood of Vice President Joe Biden being nominated. This is explained by the initial drop in the market when the difference in odds (% change Biden wins - Trump wins) was increasing during that timeframe. The volume difference between the two candidates exhibited no correlation to the probabilities of each winning, nor did it have any correlation with the market.
